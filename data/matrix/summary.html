About This Project

This project is an experiment in using agentic AI — a small pipeline of autonomous AI programs, each handling one step of the analysis — to track how each country's public posture toward the United States appears to be shifting in the wake of the U.S.–Iran War. It focuses on nations with a direct stake in that conflict.

Each time the pipeline runs, it automatically reads newly published full-text news articles about each tracked nation from The Guardian. An AI model then evaluates every article's diagnostic value — how much that one piece of evidence should shift our confidence toward or away from each of three competing explanations for that nation's stance — and adds the result to a running evidence matrix for that nation. This method, weighing each piece of evidence against several competing explanations side by side rather than looking for support of a single story, is called Analysis of Competing Hypotheses (ACH), a technique developed by veteran CIA analyst Richards Heuer to help intelligence analysts reason more rigorously and catch their own biases.

Every article is scored against the same three hypotheses:

  • H1 — [Nation] supports the United States
  • H2 — [Nation] maintains a neutral stance with the United States
  • H3 — [Nation] opposes the United States

The line graph above plots the running total of that evidence over time for each nation — it climbs when new coverage reads as pro-U.S., and falls when it reads as anti-U.S. Click any nation's line or legend entry to open its full evidence matrix: every article the system read, how it was scored against each hypothesis, and why.

This is a research and educational project (a Carnegie Mellon University Agentic AI Certificate capstone) — not an intelligence product or a forecast. It reflects one AI model's reading of Guardian news coverage, which can be noisy or incomplete, and should not be treated as ground truth or expert analysis.
